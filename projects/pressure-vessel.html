Pressure Vessel Design

Mechanical Design CAD Manufacturing

October 2025

View Repository on GitHub

Context

As part of ME421L at Duke University, I collaborated with Mason Kelsey and Dani Levine to design, machine, and test a pressure vessel capable of withstanding an internal pressure of 750 psig while containing a fluid volume between 50 and 60 cc. This project integrated mechanical design principles, stress analysis, manufacturing processes, and experimental validation.

Design & Approach

The pressure vessel design consists of a cylindrical aluminum body, a lid, and a single O-ring seal seated between the two halves. The main body was machined from a 6061 3-inch diameter aluminum rod to minimize weight while providing sufficient strength for internal pressures up to 750 psig. The design features a cylindrical body with a 3.0-inch outer diameter and a body height of 6 inches, with a central hole measuring 1 inch in diameter and 4.25 inches deep, providing an internal volume of approximately 55 cc.

The lid, measuring 3 inches in diameter and 0.750 inches thick, is fastened to the main body using four equally spaced steel bolts that compress the O-ring to create a pressure-tight seal. A 0.25 inch NPT threaded port is centered on the lid to accommodate Swagelok fittings for fluid entry and exit during testing.

Technical Implementation

Stress Analysis

Comprehensive stress analysis was performed to ensure the vessel could safely withstand the design pressure. The vessel wall thickness of 1.00 inch was analyzed using thin-wall pressure vessel theory:

  • Hoop Stress: σh = pDi/2t = 375 psi
  • Longitudinal Stress: σL = pDi/4t = 187.5 psi

With 6061-T6 aluminum yield strength of 35,000 psi, the safety factors were calculated as 93.3 for hoop stress and 186.7 for longitudinal stress, indicating the vessel easily withstands the internal test pressure.

Fastener Analysis

The axial separating force on the cover due to internal pressure was calculated as 589.05 lbf, requiring each of the four bolts to resist 147.26 lbf. Using joint stiffness analysis and a design factor of safety of 5, the required bolt preload was determined to be 736.31 lbf per bolt. Grade 8 steel bolts (1/4"-20) were selected, providing:

  • Screw yield safety factor (np): 5.10
  • Yield margin at loss of preload (nL): 36.8
  • Joint separation safety factor (n0): 5.40

O-Ring Seal Design

A high-pressure oil- and abrasion-resistant polyurethane O-ring (SAE AS568 Dash No. 128) was selected for its ability to withstand pressures up to 5,000 psi. The O-ring groove was designed following Parker O-Ring standards with:

  • Groove ID: 1.517 inches (2% stretch)
  • Groove depth: 0.07725 inches (25% compression ratio)
  • Gland width: 0.1659 inches (65% gland factor)

This face-sealing configuration ensures reliable sealing while maintaining simple machining requirements.

Design Iterations

During the machining process, several design modifications were made to improve manufacturability:

  • Lid Thickness: Reduced from 1 inch to 0.75 inches to accommodate threading tool limitations
  • Bolt Length: Reduced from 2 inches to 1.5 inches to minimize threading requirements
  • Bore Diameter: Reduced from 1.25 inches to 1.00 inches, with depth increased from 3.00 inches to 4.25 inches to maintain volume

These changes improved machining efficiency while maintaining the required internal volume and pressure capability.

Results & Outcomes

The machined pressure vessel successfully met all project requirements. Testing was performed using the laboratory pressure rig in Hudson lab, with the vessel filled with 55 mL of water. The vessel was first tested at 700 psig with no leakage or deformation observed. The pressure was then increased to 1000 psig, exceeding the design requirement of 750 psig. Even at this elevated pressure level, the vessel maintained a complete seal, confirming adequate O-ring compression, sufficient bolt preload, and proper machining tolerances.

Key Achievements

  • Designed and machined a pressure vessel meeting all specifications (750 psig, 50-60 cc volume)
  • Successfully tested to 1000 psig, exceeding design requirements by 33%
  • Performed comprehensive stress analysis with safety factors well above unity
  • Designed O-ring seal system following industry standards (Parker O-Ring Handbook)
  • Optimized design for manufacturability while maintaining performance requirements
  • Created complete CAD drawing package with assembly, part, and exploded views

Challenges & Solutions

One of the main challenges encountered during machining was threading depth limitations. The threading tool could only cut threads to a depth of about 1 inch, which was insufficient for the original 2-inch bolt design. This was solved by reducing the lid thickness to 0.75 inches and cutting the remaining 0.25 inches of thread engagement directly into the vessel body, while also reducing bolt length to 1.5 inches.

Another challenge was maintaining the required internal volume while optimizing for machining efficiency. By reducing the bore diameter and increasing depth, we achieved the same volume while reducing machining time and complexity.

Key Learnings

  • Pressure Vessel Design: Deep understanding of thin-wall pressure vessel theory and stress analysis
  • Fastener Design: Comprehensive knowledge of bolt joint analysis, preload, and joint constants
  • Seal Design: Practical experience with O-ring selection and groove design following industry standards
  • Manufacturing: Understanding of machining constraints and design-for-manufacturability principles
  • Testing & Validation: Experience with pressure testing procedures and safety protocols
  • CAD Documentation: Created complete engineering drawing package with proper dimensioning and tolerances
