Cyber-Physical Systems Lab

Research ROS2 Drones

May 2025

Context

At Duke University's Cyber-Physical Systems Lab, I have been working on UAV development that combines mechanical design, embedded systems, and ground control integration. The project aims to create a compact and flexible drone platform for controlled lab experiments as well as more advanced autonomy research.

Design & Approach

The project required a holistic approach integrating hardware modifications, software development, and system integration. The goal was to create a research platform that could operate in motion capture spaces while supporting advanced autonomy algorithms through ROS 2 integration.

Technical Implementation

Hardware Development

On the hardware side, I machined and end-milled shortened carbon fiber arms for the X500 drone, reducing its footprint to make it easier to operate in motion capture spaces. I also assisted with structural modifications to improve payload integration and durability. The carbon fiber arms were carefully designed to maintain structural integrity while reducing the overall drone footprint.

Software Integration

On the software side, I developed custom ROS 2 nodes and integrated micro-ROS with the XRCE-DDS agent to allow communication between microcontrollers and the onboard computer. This setup made it possible to control the drone with a PS4 joystick, while tracking position with a Vicon motion capture system. We validated this workflow in Gazebo simulation before transitioning to live flight tests.

Flight Control System

For flight control, the drone was configured with the PX4 firmware and connected to QGroundControl on Linux workstations. QGroundControl provided real-time telemetry, mission planning, and manual override capabilities, while ROS 2 handled higher-level autonomy and system integration. This architecture allowed for both manual control and autonomous operation.

Results & Outcomes

The project successfully created a flexible research platform that integrates mechanical design, embedded systems, and ground control. The shortened arms reduced the drone footprint by approximately 30%, making it suitable for motion capture spaces. The ROS 2 integration enabled seamless communication between all system components, and the Gazebo simulation validation ensured a smooth transition to live flight tests.

Key Achievements

  • Designed and machined shortened carbon fiber arms, reducing drone footprint by ~30%
  • Developed custom ROS 2 nodes for system integration
  • Integrated micro-ROS with XRCE-DDS agent for microcontroller communication
  • Validated system in Gazebo simulation before live testing
  • Configured PX4 firmware and QGroundControl for flight operations
  • Enabled PS4 joystick control with Vicon motion capture position tracking
